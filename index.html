01Scientific Method & Lab Safety

The scientific method is the backbone of every lab you will do. Every experiment begins with an observation, moves to a testable hypothesis, and ends with data that either supports or fails to support that hypothesis. You never "prove" a hypothesis; you support it or reject it.

Steps of the Scientific Method

The process flows from observation to question to hypothesis to experiment to data analysis to conclusion. A good hypothesis is testable, falsifiable, and written as an if/then statement. The independent variable is what you change, the dependent variable is what you measure, and controlled variables are everything you keep the same.

Mnemonic
Oh Quit Harassing Every Dog, Cat for Observation, Question, Hypothesis, Experiment, Data, Conclusion.
TermDefinitionExample
Independent variableWhat you intentionally changeAmount of fertilizer
Dependent variableWhat you measure in responsePlant height
Control groupGroup with no treatment (baseline)Plants with no fertilizer
Experimental groupGroup that receives the treatmentPlants with fertilizer
ConstantsVariables kept the same across groupsLight, water, soil type
Positive controlGroup expected to produce a known resultKnown enzyme + substrate
Negative controlGroup expected to show no responseWater instead of enzyme

Lab Safety Essentials

Always wear closed-toe shoes and safety goggles when working with chemicals. Never eat or drink in the lab. Know the location of the eyewash station, fire extinguisher, and emergency shower before the lab begins. Broken glass goes in the designated glass disposal box, never in the regular trash. When heating liquids in a test tube, point the opening away from yourself and others.

Key point: A theory is not a guess. In science, a theory is a well-tested, widely accepted explanation supported by extensive evidence (e.g., cell theory, theory of evolution). A hypothesis is the testable guess.

02Microscopy

Microscopes are fundamental tools in biology lab. You need to know how to calculate total magnification, how to properly carry and focus a microscope, and the differences between the main types of microscopes you will encounter.

Total Magnification
Total Magnification = Ocular Lens Power x Objective Lens Power

Standard ocular lens is 10x. So a 40x objective gives you 400x total.
ObjectiveMagnificationTotal (with 10x ocular)Use
Scanning4x40xFind the specimen
Low power10x100xOverview of tissue
High power40x400xCellular detail
Oil immersion100x1000xBacteria, fine detail

Types of Microscopes

TypeUsesKey Feature
Compound lightLiving/stained cells, thin sectionsUses visible light; up to ~1000x
Dissecting (stereo)Whole organisms, dissectionsLower mag, 3D view
TEMInternal cell ultrastructure2D image; up to 500,000x
SEMSurface detail3D image of surfaces

As magnification increases, the field of view decreases and the image gets dimmer. If a specimen appears in the upper right and you want to center it, move the slide to the upper right because the image is inverted and reversed.

Always start on the lowest objective when focusing a new slide. Use the coarse adjustment knob only on scanning/low power. On high power, use fine adjustment only.

03Metric System & Measurement

Biology uses the metric (SI) system exclusively. You need to convert between units fluently and understand which units apply to which measurements.

PrefixSymbolFactorExample
kilo-k1,0001 kg = 1,000 g
centi-c0.011 m = 100 cm
milli-m0.0011 L = 1,000 mL
micro-μ0.0000011 mm = 1,000 μm
nano-n0.0000000011 μm = 1,000 nm
Temperature Conversion
°C = (°F - 32) x 5/9
°F = (°C x 9/5) + 32

Water freezes at 0°C (32°F) and boils at 100°C (212°F). Human body temp ≈ 37°C.

When reading a graduated cylinder, always read the volume at the bottom of the meniscus at eye level. Record measurements to one decimal place beyond the smallest graduation on the instrument.

04Biological Molecules

There are four major classes of biological macromolecules. Each is built from smaller monomers joined by dehydration synthesis (removes water) and broken apart by hydrolysis (adds water).

MacromoleculeMonomerElementsFunctionLab Test
CarbohydratesMonosaccharidesC, H, OQuick energy, structureBenedict's (sugars), Iodine (starch)
LipidsGlycerol + fatty acidsC, H, OLong-term energy, membranesBrown paper bag test
ProteinsAmino acidsC, H, O, N, (S)Enzymes, structure, transportBiuret (turns purple)
Nucleic acidsNucleotidesC, H, O, N, PGenetic informationDische diphenylamine
Lab Test Results
Benedict's: blue (neg) to orange/red (pos for simple sugars, must heat). Iodine: yellow-brown (neg) to blue-black (pos for starch). Biuret: blue (neg) to purple (pos for protein).

Key Details

Monosaccharides include glucose, fructose, and galactose. Disaccharides: sucrose (glucose + fructose), lactose (glucose + galactose), maltose (glucose + glucose). Polysaccharides: starch (plant storage), glycogen (animal storage), cellulose (plant cell walls), chitin (arthropod exoskeletons, fungal walls).

Saturated fats have no double bonds, are straight, and are solid at room temperature. Unsaturated fats have double bonds, are kinked, and are liquid at room temperature. Phospholipids form cell membranes with hydrophilic heads and hydrophobic tails.

Protein structure has four levels: primary (amino acid sequence), secondary (alpha helices, beta sheets), tertiary (3D folding from R-group interactions), and quaternary (multiple polypeptide chains). Denaturing destroys shape, not peptide bonds.

Bond Reactions
Dehydration synthesis: monomer + monomer → polymer + H₂O
Hydrolysis: polymer + H₂O → monomer + monomer

06Diffusion & Osmosis

Diffusion is the movement of molecules from high to low concentration (down the gradient), requiring no energy. Osmosis is the diffusion of water specifically, across a selectively permeable membrane.

07Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts (almost always proteins) that speed up reactions without being consumed. They lower activation energy. Each enzyme is specific to its substrate (lock and key / induced fit). The substrate binds at the active site, forming an enzyme-substrate complex.

FactorEffect on Activity
TemperatureIncreases rate to optimum, then denatures. Human enzymes: ~37°C
pHEach has an optimum. Most: ~7. Pepsin: ~2. Trypsin: ~8
Substrate concentrationIncreases rate until all active sites saturated (Vmax)
Competitive inhibitorBlocks the active site (competes with substrate)
Noncompetitive inhibitorBinds allosteric site, changes enzyme shape
Enzyme Reaction
E + S → ES complex → E + P
E = enzyme, S = substrate, P = product. Enzyme is recycled.

Enzymes end in "-ase" (lipase, protease, amylase). Denaturation is usually irreversible and means the enzyme loses its 3D shape.

Lab connection: The catalase lab uses liver + H₂O₂. Catalase breaks H₂O₂ into water and O₂ gas (the bubbles). Boiled liver shows no reaction because the enzyme is denatured.

08Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy (glucose). It occurs in chloroplasts and has two main stages: light-dependent reactions and the Calvin cycle.

Overall Equation
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
StageLocationInputsOutputs
Light reactionsThylakoid membraneH₂O, light, ADP, NADP+O₂, ATP, NADPH
Calvin cycleStromaCO₂, ATP, NADPHG3P (makes glucose)

Chlorophyll a absorbs red and blue light while reflecting green. Accessory pigments (chlorophyll b, carotenoids, xanthophylls) absorb other wavelengths and pass energy to chlorophyll a. Paper chromatography separates pigments by solubility.

Remember
Photosynthesis is the opposite of cellular respiration. Products of one are reactants of the other. O₂ comes from splitting water, not from CO₂.
Lab connection: The floating leaf disk assay measures photosynthesis rate. Leaf disks sink, then float as O₂ fills air spaces. More light = faster floating. Dark disks serve as the control.

09Cellular Respiration

Cellular respiration extracts ATP from glucose in three main stages.

Overall Equation
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ~36-38 ATP
StageLocationInputsATP Yield
GlycolysisCytoplasmGlucose2 ATP (net)
Krebs cycleMitochondrial matrixAcetyl-CoA2 ATP
Electron transport chainInner mito. membraneNADH, FADH₂, O₂32-34 ATP

Glycolysis is anaerobic and occurs in all living cells. The Krebs cycle and ETC are aerobic. Without oxygen, cells use fermentation instead.

Fermentation TypeProductsOrganisms
AlcoholicEthanol + CO₂Yeast, some bacteria
Lactic acidLactic acidMuscle cells, some bacteria

Fermentation produces only 2 ATP per glucose (from glycolysis). Its purpose is to regenerate NAD+ so glycolysis can continue.

Lab connection: Respirometer lab uses germinating seeds. KOH absorbs CO₂, so only O₂ change is measured. Water moves toward seeds as O₂ is consumed. Temperature increases the rate.

10Mitosis & Meiosis

Mitosis produces identical cells for growth and repair. Meiosis produces gametes with half the chromosome number.

The Cell Cycle

Interphase (G1, S, G2) takes ~90% of the cell cycle. DNA replication happens in S phase.

Mitosis PhaseKey Events
ProphaseChromatin condenses, nuclear envelope breaks down, spindle forms
MetaphaseChromosomes align at the metaphase plate (middle)
AnaphaseSister chromatids separate, pulled to opposite poles
TelophaseNuclear envelopes reform, chromosomes decondense
CytokinesisCleavage furrow (animals) or cell plate (plants)
Mnemonic
PMAT for Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase. Metaphase = Middle.

Mitosis vs. Meiosis

FeatureMitosisMeiosis
Divisions12
Daughter cells2 identical (diploid)4 unique (haploid)
Chromosome #2n → 2n2n → n
Crossing overNoYes (prophase I)
Independent assortmentNoYes (metaphase I)
PurposeGrowth, repairGamete production
Key distinction: Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes (reduction). Meiosis II separates sister chromatids (like mitosis). Crossing over + independent assortment + random fertilization = genetic variation.

12DNA & Molecular Biology

DNA is a double helix of nucleotides. Each nucleotide has a phosphate group, deoxyribose sugar, and nitrogenous base. Bases pair by complementary base pairing: A-T (2 H-bonds), G-C (3 H-bonds). Strands run antiparallel (5' to 3' and 3' to 5').

Base Pairing
A-T, G-C. "Apples in the Tree, Car in the Garage." In RNA, uracil (U) replaces thymine.
FeatureDNARNA
SugarDeoxyriboseRibose
StrandsDoubleSingle
BasesA, T, G, CA, U, G, C
FunctionStores genetic infoCarries/translates info

Central Dogma

ProcessWhat HappensLocationKey Enzyme
ReplicationDNA → DNANucleusDNA polymerase
TranscriptionDNA → mRNANucleusRNA polymerase
TranslationmRNA → ProteinRibosomeRibosome, tRNA

Start codon: AUG (methionine). Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA. Point mutations change a single base; frameshift mutations (insertions/deletions) shift the entire reading frame.

Lab connection: Gel electrophoresis separates DNA by size (smaller = farther). DNA migrates toward the positive electrode (negative phosphate backbone). DNA extraction uses detergent (break membranes), salt (clump proteins), cold ethanol (precipitate DNA).

13Ecology & Populations

Energy flows one direction through ecosystems: producers to consumers to decomposers. Only about 10% transfers between trophic levels; the rest is lost as heat.

Trophic LevelRoleExample
ProducerAutotrophGrass, algae
Primary consumerHerbivoreRabbit
Secondary consumerCarnivoreSnake
Tertiary consumerTop predatorHawk
DecomposerBreaks down dead matterBacteria, fungi
InteractionEffectExample
Mutualism+/+Bee and flower
Commensalism+/0Barnacle on whale
Parasitism+/−Tick on deer
Competition−/−Two species, same niche

Exponential growth (J-curve) occurs with unlimited resources. Logistic growth (S-curve) levels off at carrying capacity (K). Density-dependent factors (competition, disease) scale with population; density-independent factors (fire, weather) do not.

Lincoln-Peterson Index (Mark-Recapture)
N = (M x C) / R
N = estimated population, M = marked first capture, C = total second capture, R = recaptured marked.

14Evolution & Natural Selection

Evolution is the change in allele frequencies in a population over time. Natural selection requires variation, heritability, overproduction, and differential survival/reproduction.

EvidenceExplanationExample
Fossil recordTransitional formsTiktaalik
Comparative anatomyHomologous structuresWhale flipper, human arm
EmbryologySimilar developmentVertebrate gill slits
Molecular biologyDNA similarityHumans/chimps ~98% DNA
BiogeographyDistribution patternsGalapagos finches
TermDefinition
Homologous structuresSame structure, different function (common ancestor)
Analogous structuresDifferent structure, same function (convergent evolution)
Vestigial structuresReduced, non-functional remnants
Adaptive radiationOne species diversifies into many
Genetic driftRandom allele change (small populations)
Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium
p + q = 1 (allele frequencies)
p² + 2pq + q² = 1 (genotype frequencies)

p = dominant allele freq, q = recessive. Equilibrium requires: no mutation, no selection, no migration, random mating, large population.
